There's a fundamental problem with your CER calculation — not with the formula itself, but with what you're comparing. Let me explain:

The Core Problem: Mismatched Corpora
Your ground truth has 1,490 regions from ~135 pages, but your OCR corpus has 26,941 regions from ~2,500 pages. You're concatenating all text from both into two long strings and comparing them — but jiwer's CER treats this as a sequential alignment problem. With the OCR text being ~18× longer, the aligner sees almost everything in the OCR as spurious insertions, which is exactly what you got: 956,113 insertions vs. only 4,563 substitutions.

This is why you got a CER of 1,552% — it's not measuring transcription quality, it's measuring the size mismatch.

What Transkribus Actually Does
Transkribus compares the automatic transcription of the same pages against the correct original transcription for those same pages, then counts incorrect characters as a percentage of the total characters in ground truth. The key is page-for-page alignment — the same 135 pages, compared against each other. 
transkribus

How to Fix It
You need to compare only the OCR output for the same ~135 pages that are in your ground truth, not the entire 2,500-page corpus.

# Step 1: identify which source files are in the ground truth
gt_files = set(df_gt["source_file"].unique())

# Step 2: filter the OCR dataframe to only those files
df_ocr_matched = df_ocr[df_ocr["source_file"].isin(gt_files)]

# Step 3: now compute CER on matched text
gt_text  = " ".join(df_gt["text"].dropna().tolist())
ocr_text = " ".join(df_ocr_matched["text"].dropna().tolist())

The Formula Itself is Correct
The jiwer CER formula matches Transkribus exactly: CER = (i + s + d) / n, where n is the total number of characters in the ground truth. So once you're comparing the right pages, the code is fine. 
transkribus

In short: fix the input data before the calculation, and your 1,552% will likely drop to something meaningful (hopefully under 10%).
